Method

HavenIntel builds briefs around football structure, likely control patterns, match pressure, and realistic score development rather than hype or certainty language.

What the brief is trying to answer

A match brief is meant to answer three visitor-facing questions: which side looks structurally stronger, what kind of match shape is most likely, and what score environment best fits that football picture.

What is being weighed

HavenIntel may weigh team strength, control in midfield, defensive stability, player availability, travel or preparation noise, likely tempo, and how much the first goal could change the emotional balance of the game.

Why score-range language is used

Score-range language is used to narrow the likely scoreboard paths, not to pretend the match can be predicted perfectly. It is one layer of structured opinion inside a wider football case.

How the review works

Once a match is finished, HavenIntel moves it into the archive and reviews what held, what missed, and whether the pre-match logic actually described the game that arrived. That review layer is how the method is tested in public.
